Tuesday's lecture shaded a surface from three directions: the light L, the normal N, and the eye V, summed into diffuse (max(0, N·L)), specular (max(0, R·V)^s), and a constant ambient floor. Part A · Shader studio ~50 min

0 · Get the Topic9 projects

The projects are in the course materials on Canvas (Topic9-Illumination). Download, unzip somewhere without spaces in the path, and add each folder in Unity Hub (Projects → Add → Add project from disk). Open with the pinned editor from Lab 01. Today's projects:

  • 9.2.AnalyzingTheNormal, 9.3.DiffuseIllumination, and 9.4.PointLightSource each open and compile without errors.

1 · Visualize the normal

Goal. See the normal the lighting depends on, and see why a wrong one wrecks shading. Open 9.2.AnalyzingTheNormal. In 451NoCullShader.shader, find the commented debug line in frag that returns the normal as a color: return fixed4(i.normal, 1);.

POKE 1a · NORMAL AS COLOR

Paint the normals

Uncomment the return fixed4(i.normal, 1); line (comment out the normal shading return), save, and look at the surface: each pixel's color is its world-space normal.

Predict first: a face whose normal points along +x shows up reddish, along +y greenish, along +z bluish (the normal's components are the r,g,b). Predict which color the top of the model takes before you look, then confirm. Where the color changes smoothly, the normal is turning smoothly.
POKE 1b · THE NORMAL TRANSFORM

Read the hand normal-transform

In the vertex stage, read the block that builds o.normal from v.vertex + v.normal pushed through UNITY_MATRIX_M and subtracted — with the comment "we don't have access to inverse-transpose".

Predict first: lecture said normals need the inverse-transpose (M⁻¹)ᵀ, not M. This shader lacks it, so it transforms a point and a point-plus-normal by the same M and subtracts to recover the direction. Predict: for a pure rotation, does this give the right normal? For a non-uniform scale, is it exact or approximate? Reason it out, then discuss with a neighbor.

2 · Read the diffuse term

Goal. Confirm max(0, N·L) on a real surface. Open 9.3.DiffuseIllumination and read ComputeDiffuse: it returns clamp(dot(i.normal, l), 0, 1) — exactly the clamped cosine from lecture.

Where to look: ComputeDiffuse builds l = normalize(LightPosition - i.vertexWC) (the direction to the light, our L) and returns clamp(dot(i.normal, l), 0, 1). That clamp(…, 0, 1) is the lecture's max(0, N·L) — back faces get zero.
POKE 2a · MOVE THE LIGHT

Watch the falloff

Select the light object and move it around the surface (or rotate the surface).

Predict first: the surface is brightest where it faces the light (N·L near 1) and fades to black as it turns past 90° (the clamp pins it at 0). Predict where the dark terminator line falls for a given light position, then move the light and confirm the gradient is smooth, not a hard edge. (Recall the lecture number: at the marked point with the default light, N·L = 0.988.)
POKE 2b · BREAK THE CLAMP

Remove the max, see the bug

Temporarily change clamp(dot(i.normal, l), 0, 1) to just dot(i.normal, l) (no lower clamp). Save and look at the back-facing side.

Predict first: without the max(0, ·), back faces have a negative N·L. Predict what a negative diffuse looks like on screen (and why it is wrong), then confirm. Restore the clamp afterward.

3 · Tweak the spotlight cone

Goal. See a spotlight as diffuse × an angular mask. Open 9.4.PointLightSource and read ComputeDiffuse: it computes alpha = acos(dot(l, LightDirection)) (the angle to the spot's aim) and fades between _MinTheta and _MaxTheta with smoothstep, then returns ndotl * strength.

Where to look: PointLight.cs pushes LightPosition, LightColor, LightDirection, SlightPos, Near/Far into shader globals via LoadLightToShader(); LoadLight.cs calls it every frame. The _MinTheta / _MaxTheta cone angles are exposed on the material in the Inspector.
POKE 3a · CONE WIDTH

Widen and narrow the beam

Select the material and change _MaxTheta (the outer cone). Try a small value, then a larger one.

Predict first: alpha is the angle from the beam center; strength is full inside _MinTheta, zero past _MaxTheta, and a smoothstep fade between. Predict what raising _MaxTheta does to the lit circle's size, and what widening the gap _MaxTheta - _MinTheta does to its edge (soft vs hard). Then adjust both and confirm.
POKE 3b · THE MASKED DIFFUSE

Diffuse times the cone

The function returns ndotl * strength — the ordinary diffuse cosine multiplied by the cone mask.

Predict first: a point can be inside the cone (strength = 1) yet still dark if it faces away from the light (ndotl = 0). Predict what the surface looks like where the cone hits a steeply-angled face, then find such a spot and confirm. Which lecture term is ndotl? Which is strength?

Part B · Final Project progress demos ~50 min

Each team gives a short, timed progress demo of its Final Project and gets rapid feedback from the room. This is a checkpoint, not a final grade — the goal is to surface blockers early and trade ideas while there is still time to act on them.

The running order, per-team timebox, and the feedback form are on Canvas (the single source of truth). MP6 is also due this week — dates, points, and requirements are on Canvas. Nothing about scheduling or grading is invented here.

Running order & timing

  • Order & timebox: see the Canvas running order — teams present in that sequence, each within its posted time slot. Watch the clock; a hard timebox keeps every team on the schedule.
  • Split your slot roughly: most of it showing something running, a little setup, and leave room at the end for feedback.
  • Feedback: while each team presents, the others fill in the Canvas feedback form — one concrete strength, one concrete suggestion. Specific beats polite.
PRESENTERS

What to show in your slot

  • Show it running — even a rough, partial build beats slides. Lead with the thing that works.
  • Name your current blocker — the one problem you would most like help with. The room is your rubber duck.
  • State what is next — the concrete next step you will take before the project is due.
Tie it to the course: if your project shades surfaces, say which lighting terms you use (diffuse? specular? how many lights?) and where your normals come from — exactly tonight's Part 2 and Part 3. A specific technical question gets you the best help of the week.
AUDIENCE

How to give useful feedback

For each team, on the Canvas form: one thing that clearly works (be specific — "the camera control feels smooth," not "nice job") and one actionable suggestion ("try clamping the diffuse — the back faces look wrong"). Vague praise helps no one; a concrete pointer might unblock a team.

Before you leave

  • You visualized the normal in 9.2 and reasoned about the hand normal-transform.
  • You read max(0, N·L) in 9.3 and saw what breaks without the clamp.
  • You tweaked the spotlight cone in 9.4 and identified which term is the diffuse and which is the mask.
  • Your team gave its timed FP progress demo, and you submitted feedback on Canvas for the other teams.
  • You checked Canvas for the MP6 due date and requirements.
