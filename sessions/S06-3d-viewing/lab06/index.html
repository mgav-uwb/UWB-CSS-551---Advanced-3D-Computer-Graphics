Lab 06 — CameraMatrices: build, break & predict the view

Week 6 · Thursday in-person studio · Dr. Marcel Gavriliu · bring your laptop

Tuesday's lecture built the two matrices that turn a placed 3D world into pixels — the view matrix V (rows are the camera basis u,v,w; the translation is −basis·eye) and the perspective matrix P (divide by depth). Today you run Sung's CameraMatrices project, switch through all five ViewMatrixMode constructions, and break the view on purpose — predicting what each mode does before you press Play. The second half is MP5 kickoff — the camera-manipulation machine.

Predict before you run — every time. The whole point of the studio is to catch the moment your mental model and the screen disagree. If you press Play first, you have learned nothing. Say the answer out loud or jot it, then run.

1 · Read the five view-matrix modes

Goal. See that four of the five modes build the same view matrix by different routes, and one is deliberately wrong. Open CameraMatrices.cs and read the switch (ViewMatrixCompute) block:

ComputeFromScratch:  W = Cross(V,U); U = Cross(W,V); r.SetRow(0,W); r.SetRow(1,U); r.SetRow(2,V);
SetMatrixRow:        r.SetRow(0,right); r.SetRow(1,up); r.SetRow(2,-forward);   // basis in the ROWS
SetMatrixCol:        r.SetColumn(0,right); ...; r = r.inverse;                  // basis in COLUMNS, then invert
UseTransformRotate:  r = TRS(0, localRotation, 1); r.SetColumn(2,-col2); r = r.inverse;
ViewMatrixWrong:     break;   // r stays IDENTITY — no rotation at all
Why four match: for a rotation matrix, "rows = basis" (SetMatrixRow) equals "columns = basis, then invert" (SetMatrixCol), because a rotation's inverse is its transpose. The other two reach the same place through Unity's transform. ViewMatrixWrong throws the basis away.
CHECKPOINT 1a

Predict the four "correct" modes

You will switch ViewMatrixCompute between the four non-wrong modes and watch the scene.

Predict first: the view matrix maps world → camera; all four correct modes build the same rotation (basis as rows, or its transpose-inverse). Predict: as you switch ComputeFromScratch → SetMatrixRow → SetMatrixCol → UseTransformRotate, does the rendered image change at all? Write your prediction, then switch through all four and confirm the picture holds steady — four routes, one matrix.
CHECKPOINT 1b

Row vs column, by hand

On paper, write the camera basis as rows (SetMatrixRow) and as columns-then-inverted (SetMatrixCol).

Predict first: for an orthonormal basis, the inverse is the transpose. Predict the relationship between the row-form matrix and the column-form-inverted matrix (hint: they should be identical). State why in one sentence, then confirm both dropdown choices render the same view.

2 · Break it: ViewMatrixWrong

Goal. Prove the basis rows are load-bearing by removing them. Switch ViewMatrixCompute to ViewMatrixWrong — the empty case that leaves the rotation as identity.

CHECKPOINT 2a

Predict what "wrong" looks like

With no basis in the rotation, only the −position translation survives.

Predict first: the camera still sits at the right place (the translation is intact) but never rotates to face the target — it stares down world −z forever. Predict what the scene looks like: is anything on screen? Is it aimed at the objects, or off to the side? Sketch it, then switch to ViewMatrixWrong and compare. This is the moment the cross products prove they were doing something.
CHECKPOINT 2b

Re-aim and confirm the failure

Still in ViewMatrixWrong, rotate the camera object (change where it points) in the Scene view.

Predict first: a correct view matrix would re-frame the scene as you turn the camera. In ViewMatrixWrong, the rendered image should not respond to aiming, because the rotation is thrown away. Predict: does turning the camera change the picture? Confirm, then switch back to ComputeFromScratch and watch aiming work again — that difference is exactly the SetRow-of-the-basis lines.

3 · Projection: perspective vs orthographic

Goal. Connect the fov/near/far sliders to the frustum you squeezed in Tuesday's demo, and see the divide-by-depth appear and disappear. In the camera component, switch ProjectionMode and edit the camera's Field of View / clip planes.

Where to look: CameraMatrices.cs builds Matrix4x4.Perspective(fov, aspect, near, far) for Perspective and Matrix4x4.Ortho(...) for Orthographics, then Shader.SetGlobalMatrix("CameraProjMatrix", p). The companion 6.4.DrawCameraFrustum draws the frustum with corner height 2·near·tan(fov/2) — the same formula the lecture demo used.
CHECKPOINT 3a

Predict the frustum squeeze

Stay in Perspective. Widen the camera's Field of View, then push the near clip plane outward.

Predict first: widening fov flares the frustum (more of the scene fits, objects look smaller); pushing near out slices off close geometry. Predict which objects appear or disappear for each edit, then make the edits and confirm. Relate the near-plane slice to Tuesday's marked box flipping from INSIDE to OUTSIDE.
CHECKPOINT 3b

Predict: perspective vs orthographic

Switch ProjectionMode from Perspective to Orthographics.

Predict first: perspective divides by depth, so far objects shrink and parallel lines converge; orthographic does not divide, so size is constant and parallels stay parallel. Predict how the same scene looks different under the two modes (which one looks like a CAD drawing?). Switch and confirm, then explain in one sentence which matrix entry — the −1 in the perspective w-row — causes the divide.

4 · MP5 kickoff — the camera-manipulation machine

MP5 is the camera-manipulation machine problem — interactive camera controls built on the eye/at/up frame and the view matrix you built Tuesday. The three moves are the ones from lecture:

  • tumble (orbit) — swing eye around at on a sphere (azimuth + elevation)
  • track (pan) — slide eye and at together, sideways
  • dolly — move eye toward / away from at along the sightline

Each move edits the frame, then rebuilds V — exactly lookAtBasis from lecture. Sung's 6.3.Tumble CameraManipulation shows one of them (a pivot sandwich orbiting the eye about the target); open it and trace how it moves eye, then re-aims.

Get MP5 open now and make a start: sketch which mouse gesture drives which move, decide how each gesture changes eye/at, and wire the first control (tumble is the natural first). Pull the instructor over with a specific blocker — this is the best help-per-minute of the week.
