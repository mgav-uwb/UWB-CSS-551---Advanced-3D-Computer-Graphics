Lab 03 — Projection: shadows, reflection & cylinders

Week 3 · Thursday in-person studio · Dr. Marcel Gavriliu · bring your laptop

Tuesday's lecture derived rotation from last week's projection split: to spin a vector about an axis you peel it into the piece along the axis and the piece across it. Today's three exercises are pure projection — EX4 (shadow on a plane), EX5 (reflection across a plane), and EX6 (project onto a cylinder). Each one splits a point into an along-a-direction part and an across-it part, exactly the move behind Rodrigues. Work at your own pace; the instructor is circulating.

The course rule is in force. Build from the primitive operations only: Vector3.Dot, Vector3.Cross, .normalized, .magnitude, component access, and vector + - *. Do not reach for Vector3.Project, Vector3.ProjectOnPlane, Vector3.Reflect, or Vector3.Angle — building those by hand is the exercise.

The three exercise skeletons are provided in the course materials on Canvas (Topic3-InClassCodingExercises). Download, unzip somewhere without spaces in the path, and add the folder in Unity Hub (Projects → Add → Add project from disk). Open it with the pinned editor from Lab 01.

  • Project opens and compiles without errors.

  • Each exercise is its own scene — open the matching scene before you press Play.
  • The plane convention (stated in every file) is n · P = D, i.e. n · P − D = 0.

1 · EX4 — Shadow on a plane

Goal. Drop a point P straight onto the white plane along the plane's normal, show the landing spot with ShadowPoint, and color P green when it is in front of the plane and red when behind. This is the parallel/perpendicular split from lecture Part 2, applied to a plane.

Allowed API: WhitePlane.up (the unit normal n), Vector3.Dot, P.localPosition, WhitePlane.localPosition (a point on the plane), component access, vector + - *, and the color setter from the file (GetComponent<Renderer>().material.color). The helper Support.UpdatePlaneNormal is already wired.
CHECKPOINT 4a

Project P onto the infinite plane

The shadow of P is P minus the part of it that sticks out along the normal. Compute the signed distance P is off the plane (n · P − D), then step back along n by that much. Set ShadowPoint to the result.

Predict first: the shadow is P − (n · P − D) * n. Before running, work out on paper what D is — it is n · Q for a known point Q on the plane (its position). If you forgot the − D term and used P − (n · P) * n, would the shadow land on the plane, or on a parallel plane through the origin? When would you not notice the bug?
CHECKPOINT 4b

Color P by which side it is on

Reuse the signed distance from 4a. Its sign is the which-side test: positive means P is on the normal's side (in front). Set P's color green in front, red behind.

Predict first: before you move anything, predict the color for a point clearly above the plane, then clearly below. Now drag P slowly through the plane — at the exact instant the color flips, what is the value of n · P − D? Confirm the shadow point sits under your cursor the whole time.

2 · EX5 — Reflection across a plane

Goal. A line runs from P1 to P2 and pierces the white plane at Pt. Find Pt, then reflect the segment across the plane's normal — but only reflect when P2 is in front of the plane. Color P2 green when it reflects, red when it does not.

Allowed API: WhitePlane.up, Vector3.Dot, P1.localPosition / P2.localPosition, .normalized, .magnitude, vector + - *, and the given Support.UpdateLine / Support.CreateLine helpers. Not Vector3.Reflect — build the reflection yourself.
CHECKPOINT 5a

Find where the line crosses the plane

Walk the segment P1 → P2 and stop where the signed distance hits zero. The crossing parameter is t = (D − n · P1) / (n · (P2 − P1)); then Pt = P1 + t (P2 − P1).

Predict first: what does the denominator n · (P2 − P1) measure, and what does it mean geometrically when it is zero? Predict what should happen to Pt when the line runs parallel to the plane — and decide how your code will guard that case before you run it.
CHECKPOINT 5b

Reflect the incoming direction across the normal

Take the incoming direction d = (P2 − P1).normalized. Split it into its along-normal part and its in-plane part, then flip the along-normal part. The reflected direction keeps the in-plane part and negates the normal part.

Predict first: the reflection is d − 2 (d · n) n. Expand it as "(in-plane part) − (along-normal part)" and convince yourself on paper it matches the split. If you dropped the factor of 2 and used d − (d · n) n, what would you get instead — and what does that vector already have a name for from EX4?
CHECKPOINT 5c

Reflect only from the front; color P2

Draw the reflected segment starting at Pt. Reflect only when P2 is in front of the plane (n · P2 − D > 0); color P2 green when it reflects, red when it does not.

Predict first: which reused quantity from EX4 decides front-vs-behind here? Move P2 from front to back through the plane and predict the moment the green segment disappears.

3 · EX6 — Project onto a cylinder

Goal. Project a point P onto the surface of a vertical cylinder: drop P onto the cylinder's axis, push out to the radius, and mark the spot with Pt. If the projection falls outside the cylinder's height, color it red; inside, color it blue. Same split — along the axis vs. across it.

Allowed API: TheCylinder.up (the axis direction vn), TheCylinder.localPosition, CylinderRadius, Vector3.Dot, .normalized, .magnitude, vector + - *, and the color setter. CP1/CP2 (the axis endpoints) and the cylinder scale are already computed in the skeleton's Update.
CHECKPOINT 6a

Drop P onto the cylinder's axis

Project P onto the axis line to get the foot point F: with the axis passing through the cylinder center C in unit direction vn, F = C + (Dot(P − C, vn)) * vn.

Predict first: this is the point-to-line projection from Week 2. What is Dot(P − C, vn) measuring, in what units, and what does its sign tell you about whether P is above or below the cylinder's center? Match it to the height check you will need in 6c.
CHECKPOINT 6b

Push out to the surface

The across-axis part is P − F. Normalize it and step out by CylinderRadius from F: Pt = F + CylinderRadius * (P − F).normalized.

Predict first: P − F is the perpendicular from the axis to P — the same v-perp idea as lecture Part 2. What happens to (P − F).normalized when P sits exactly on the axis, and how will you keep it from blowing up?
CHECKPOINT 6c

Color by inside vs. outside the height

Compare the along-axis distance from 6a against the cylinder's half-height. Color Pt blue when the foot point F lies between the two endpoints, red when it runs off either end.

Predict first: the height test is a clamp on the same scalar you found in 6a (Week 2's segment test). Slide P up past the top cap — predict the exact height at which Pt turns from blue to red.
