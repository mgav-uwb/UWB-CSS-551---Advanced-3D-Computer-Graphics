Part A · Per-team checkpoint ~55 min

Work through the checkpoint with your team. Each item is a question to answer honestly about where your project actually stands right now — not where you hope it will be. Anything you cannot check is a gap; the rest of the studio is for closing gaps, so surface them early and flag the instructor.

1 · It runs

  • Your project builds and runs on the machine you will demo from — from a clean checkout / fresh open, not just "it works if I don't touch it."
  • You have a known-good starting state: a scene, level, or view that reliably comes up, so a demo never opens on a crash or a black screen.
  • You can recover if something breaks mid-demo (a reset key, a reload, a backup build) — rehearse the recovery, not just the happy path.

2 · It shows the pipeline you built

Your project should visibly exercise the pipeline from this course. Confirm which pieces yours uses — you will be asked to name them at the demo (this is the connection to the whole quarter, Lecture 10's Part 4).

  • Transforms & placement — objects placed with model/world matrices (composition, a scene graph, or hierarchical parts).
  • Camera — a view matrix and a projection you can point to (even if via the engine, know which is which and roughly what they do).
  • Surfaces — meshes, and, if you use them, textures / UVs.
  • Shading — if your scene is lit, know your lighting terms (diffuse? specular? how many lights?) and where your normals come from.

3 · It is demoable

  • You can show the most interesting thing your project does within the first minute — lead with the payoff, not the loading screen.
  • You have a one-sentence description of what the project is and why it is interesting.
  • You know your current blocker — the single thing you would most like to fix or get help with before the demo.
  • You have checked the Canvas deliverables list and know what must be submitted alongside the demo (code, write-up, whatever the brief requires).
DO IT NOW · GAP LIST

Turn the checkpoint into a to-do list

As a team, write down every box you could not check above. Order them: demo-blocking first (it does not run, no reliable starting state), then demo-improving (leads with the payoff, recovery path), then polish. Spend the rest of the studio top-down on that list.

Use the room: if a gap is a specific technical problem — a transform that looks wrong, a camera that clips, lighting that reads flat — the instructor circulates during studio, and a specific question ("my world matrix seems to double-apply the parent rotation") gets the fastest help. Your neighbors are your rubber duck too.

Part B · Demo-day requirements recap ~35 min

A recap of what a strong finals-week demo looks like, so you can rehearse against it today. The exact format, time limit, running order, and rubric are on Canvas — this is guidance for shaping your demo, not a substitute for the authoritative requirements there.

SHOW

Show it running

  • Live beats slides. Open with the project actually running and doing its most interesting thing. A rough live build is more convincing than a polished deck about a build.
  • Drive it deliberately — a short rehearsed path through the features, not aimless clicking.
  • Have a fallback — a short screen recording of it working, in case the live build misbehaves on the day.
EXPLAIN

Name the pipeline pieces you used

Tie it to the course. In your own words, say which stages from this quarter your project uses — how you place objects (transforms / scene graph), your camera (view & projection), your surfaces (meshes / textures), and your shading (lighting terms, normals). This is exactly Lecture 10's "the course in one picture": you should be able to point at your project and name W, V, P and what they do.
REFLECT

What is next, what was hard

  • What you would do next with more time — the concrete next feature or fix.
  • What was hard and how you worked around it — the honest engineering story is often the most interesting part.
